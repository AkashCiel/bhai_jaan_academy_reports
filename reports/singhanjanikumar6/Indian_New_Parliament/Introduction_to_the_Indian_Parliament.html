Key Concepts:

Basic Definition

Parliament: A parliamentary system is a political system where the government is directly accountable to the parliament and the parliament is composed of elected representatives.

    • Legislature: The legislative branch of government responsible for making laws.
    • Bicameral System: A legislature that has two separate chambers or houses. Indiaâ€™s Parliament consists of the Lok Sabha (House of the People) and the Rajya Sabha (Council of States).

Structure of the Indian Parliament

    • Lok Sabha (House of the People):
    • Membership: Composed of representatives directly elected by the people of India.
    • Tenure: Members serve for a term of five years unless the house is dissolved earlier.

    Powers: Primarily responsible for making laws, controlling finances, and representing the electorate.

    Rajya Sabha (Council of States):

    • Membership: Represents the states and union territories, with members elected by the elected members of the state legislative assemblies.
    • Tenure: Members serve for six years, with one-third of its members retiring every two years.

    Powers: Reviews and suggests amendments to legislation passed by the Lok Sabha.

    President of India:

    • Acts as the ceremonial head of state and plays a crucial role in the functioning of Parliament, including summoning and proroguing sessions.

Key Functions

    • Legislation: The primary function of Parliament is to enact laws. Bills can be introduced in either house, and must be passed by both houses and receive the Presidentâ€™s assent to become law.
    • Representation: Parliament represents the diverse interests of the Indian populace, serving as a platform for citizens' voices.
    • Oversight: Parliament has the power to oversee the executive branch, ensuring accountability through questioning, debates, and committees.
    • Budget Approval: The Parliament holds power over public finances, including the approval of the national budget.

Historical Context

    • Evolution: The Indian Parliament evolved from the colonial legislative councils established during British rule. The Government of India Act, 1935, was a significant step towards self-governance leading to the establishment of a full-fledged parliamentary system post-independence in 1947.
    • Constitutional Framework: The Constitution of India, adopted in 1950, laid the foundation for the parliamentary system and detailed the powers and functions of Parliament.

Real-World Applications:

Case Studies

    • The 73rd and 74th Amendments:

    These amendments empowered local self-governments in rural and urban areas, respectively, illustrating the parliamentary commitment to decentralization and grassroots democracy.

    The Goods and Services Tax (GST) Act of 2017:

    This landmark legislation unified various indirect taxes under a single regime, showcasing Parliament's role in economic reform and boosting ease of doing business.

    The Right to Information (RTI) Act of 2005:

    • This act enhanced transparency and accountability in governance, demonstrating how parliamentary legislation can empower citizens.

Practical Implications

    • Citizen Engagement: Through various mechanisms like public consultations and petitions, citizens can engage with their representatives, influencing legislative processes.
    • Policy Formulation: Parliament plays a critical role in shaping policies that impact daily life, such as education, health, and infrastructure.
