Key Concepts

The Indian Parliament

Definition: The Indian Parliament is the supreme legislative body of the Republic of India, comprising two houses: the Lok Sabha (House of the People) and the Rajya Sabha (Council of States).

Structure of the Parliament

    • Lok Sabha:
    • Composed of representatives directly elected by the people through general elections.
    • Each member serves a term of five years unless the house is dissolved earlier.

    The Lok Sabha has 543 members at present.

    Rajya Sabha:

    • Comprised of representatives elected by the state legislatures and appointed members.
    • Members serve staggered six-year terms, with one-third of the members retiring every two years.
    • Total membership is capped at 250, with 12 members nominated by the President of India for their expertise in specific fields.

Functions of Parliament

The primary functions of the Indian Parliament include:

    Legislation: The Parliament is responsible for enacting laws. A bill must pass through both houses and receive the President's assent to become law.

    Representation: Members of Parliament represent the interests and concerns of their constituents, bridging the gap between the government and the governed.

    Oversight: Parliament monitors the work of the executive branch, ensuring accountability and transparency in governance.

    Budget Control: Parliament examines and approves the budget, thus controlling public expenditure.

Federalism in India

Definition: Federalism refers to the distribution of power between a central authority and its constituent units, ensuring a division of responsibilities and autonomy.

Key Features of Indian Federalism

    Division of Powers: Powers are divided between the central government and state governments as outlined in the Constitution of India. The Constitution provides three lists of subjects: the Union List, the State List, and the Concurrent List, determining which level of government is responsible for which areas.

    Supremacy of the Constitution: The Constitution is the highest law of the land, and any law inconsistent with it is void.

    Bicameral Legislature: The Parliament has two houses, while many states have bicameral legislatures as well, allowing for representation at both state and national levels.

    Intergovernmental Relations: The Constitution also outlines mechanisms for cooperation and conflict resolution between the central and state governments.

The Connection Between Parliament and Federalism

The Indian Parliament plays a critical role in the federal structure of the country. It not only legislates on matters of national importance but also influences state governance through its powers to legislate on concurrent subjects. Moreover, the Parliament is instrumental in maintaining the balance of power between the center and the states.

Real-World Applications

The interplay between the Indian Parliament and federalism has real-world implications that affect governance and public policy.

Legislative Process in Action

Consider the Goods and Services Tax (GST) implementation in India. The GST, a landmark tax reform, required extensive collaboration between the central and state governments. The Parliament passed the GST Bill, but its success depended on the cooperation of state legislatures to enact complementary laws. This case illustrates how the federal structure necessitates coordination between different levels of government.

Role of Civil Society

Civil society organizations (CSOs) play a vital role in advocating for transparency and accountability within the parliamentary system. For example, organizations like Common Cause and Association for Democratic Reforms (ADR) actively monitor parliamentary activities, pushing for reforms that enhance democratic governance. This engagement highlights the importance of civil society in a federal framework, ensuring that diverse voices are heard within the legislative process.

Technology and Federalism

The integration of technology in parliamentary processes, such as online platforms for public feedback on proposed legislation, exemplifies how technology can enhance federalism. For instance, during the drafting of the Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Act, 2019, the Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment solicited public input through digital platforms. This initiative demonstrates how technology facilitates broader participation in governance, reinforcing democratic principles within a federal structure.

Current Challenges and Future Directions

Challenges

    Political Fragmentation: Coalition politics, as previously discussed, complicates governance at both central and state levels. The diverse interests of regional parties may lead to legislative gridlock, hindering effective policy-making.

    Accountability Issues: Despite mechanisms for oversight, instances of corruption and lack of transparency persist. Strengthening accountability measures is crucial for restoring public trust in the parliamentary system.

    Technological Barriers: While technology enhances transparency, the digital divide remains a challenge, with marginalized communities often lacking access to digital platforms for engagement.

Future Directions

    Strengthening Intergovernmental Relations: Enhanced collaboration between central and state governments is essential for effective governance. Establishing regular consultations and joint committees can facilitate better coordination.

    Leveraging Technology: Expanding digital literacy and access can empower citizens to engage more effectively with the legislative process. Initiatives aimed at bridging the digital divide will be crucial.

    Engaging Civil Society: Encouraging greater involvement of CSOs in the legislative process can enhance accountability and responsiveness. This can be achieved through formal channels for public consultation and feedback.
