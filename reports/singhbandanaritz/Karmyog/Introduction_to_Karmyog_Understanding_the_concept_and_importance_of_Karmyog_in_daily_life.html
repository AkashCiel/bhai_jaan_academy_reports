Introduction:

Karmyog, a term derived from the Sanskrit words "karma" (action) and "yog" (union or path), embodies the philosophy of selfless action and duty. It is a key concept in Hindu philosophy, primarily articulated in the Bhagavad Gita, where it is presented as a path to spiritual realization through the performance of one's duties without attachment to the fruits of those actions. This report delves into the multifaceted nature of Karmyog, examining its significance in daily life, its philosophical underpinnings, practical applications, and its relevance in the contemporary world.

Key Concepts:

Basic Definition:

Karmyog: The practice of performing one's duty or actions selflessly, without attachment to the outcomes.

    • Karma: Represents action, work, or deed. It is the principle of cause and effect, where every action has a corresponding reaction.
    • Yog: Refers to a path or discipline that leads to union with the divine or the realization of one's true self.

Philosophical Foundations:

Karmyog is rooted in the philosophy of the Bhagavad Gita, a 700-verse Hindu scripture that is part of the Indian epic Mahabharata. It presents a dialogue between Prince Arjuna and Lord Krishna, who serves as his charioteer.     Detachment from Results: By practicing detachment, individuals can avoid the stress and anxiety associated with expectations and outcomes, leading to a more peaceful state of mind.

Historical Context:

The concept of Karmyog has evolved over centuries and has influenced various philosophical schools and spiritual practices in India. Notable figures such as Mahatma Gandhi have interpreted and advocated for Karmyog as a means to social change and personal development.
